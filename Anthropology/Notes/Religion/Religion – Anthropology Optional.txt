Q1. Discuss the anthropological approaches to the study of religion, highlighting the views of E.B. Tylor, Emile Durkheim, and Clifford Geertz. (20 marks)

Answer:

Intro: Religion is a universal institution, studied by anthropologists from evolutionary, functionalist, and symbolic perspectives.

Tylor: Proposed animism as the earliest form of religion (belief in souls/spirits). He saw religion evolving → animism → polytheism → monotheism → science. His view is evolutionary.

Durkheim: Saw religion as a social fact; core of religion is distinction between sacred and profane. Rituals create collective effervescence and strengthen solidarity. Functionalist approach.

Geertz: Proposed interpretive approach – religion as a cultural system of symbols that provide meaning to existence. He emphasized lived experience rather than structure.

Conclusion: Tylor → origins, Durkheim → social function, Geertz → meaning. All together show religion is biological (psychological need), social (cohesion), and cultural (meaning).

Q2. Differentiate between Magic, Religion, and Science with anthropological examples. (15 marks)

Answer:

Intro: Magic, religion, and science are three ways humans cope with uncertainty.

Magic: Manipulative, individualistic, based on belief in direct cause-effect. Malinowski observed Trobriand Islanders using magic before risky open-sea fishing.

Religion: Collective, ritual-based, appeals to supernatural beings. Durkheim: religion sustains community solidarity.

Science: Empirical, rational, testable. Provides verifiable results.

Overlap: In tribal societies, all three co-exist (magic for risk, religion for morality, science for tools).

Conclusion: Magic addresses uncertainty, religion provides meaning, science explains reality. None is completely absent in any society.

Q3. Examine the role of rituals and myths in sustaining social order. (15 marks)

Answer:

Intro: Rituals and myths are symbolic elements of religion, crucial in transmitting values.

Myths: Narratives about origins, gods, ancestors. Example: Hindu Purusha Sukta justifies varna; tribal myths explain clan identity.

Rituals: Repetitive symbolic practices – e.g., birth, marriage, death rites. Durkheim: rituals reinforce collective conscience.

Integration: Myths legitimize authority, rituals enact and reinforce it. Example: coronation rituals of kings, tribal fertility rites.

Conclusion: Myths give meaning, rituals give action. Together they ensure continuity of social order.

Q4. Discuss the impact of religion on caste system in India. (20 marks)

Answer:

Intro: Caste and religion in India are deeply intertwined. Religion provided ideological sanction to caste.

Hinduism: Varna system in Rigveda and Purusha Sukta – caste hierarchy. Dharma and Karma theory – justified inequality.

Buddhism & Jainism: Opposed ritualism and caste hierarchy. Promoted equality, though caste persisted socially.

Bhakti & Sufi movements: Emphasized devotion and God’s unity. Saints like Kabir, Nanak challenged caste rigidities.

Modern reforms: Arya Samaj, Brahmo Samaj, Ambedkar’s Buddhism. Constitution provides equality (Articles 15, 17, 25).

Conclusion: Religion reinforced caste but also provided tools for reform. Thus, dual impact.

Q5. Write short notes on Religious Syncretism in Indian society. (10 marks)

Answer:

Intro: Syncretism means blending of traditions of different religions. India has strong history of it.

Examples:

Bhakti–Sufi synthesis (Kabir, Nanak, Chishti saints).

Tribal-Hindu mix (Sarna dharma, tribal deities incorporated into Hindu pantheon).

Christianity in Kerala adopting local customs.


Importance: Shows India’s pluralism, interfaith harmony.

Conclusion: Religious syncretism reflects adaptability of Indian culture, ensuring social cohesion amidst diversity.

Q6 (PYQ 2016 – Paper I). Discuss the concept of sacred and profane in Durkheim’s theory of religion. (15 marks)

Answer:

Intro: Durkheim studied Australian totemism, saw religion as a social fact.

Sacred: Objects/rituals set apart, inspire awe, represent community values (e.g., totem, temple, holy books).

Profane: Everyday, ordinary life.

Religion: System of beliefs uniting people around sacred. Rituals reinforce sacred/profane division.

Conclusion: Durkheim’s distinction explains why religion persists: it is less about gods and more about society worshipping itself.

Q8 (PYQ 2020 – Paper I). Examine the role of myth in the study of religion. (15 marks)

Answer:

Intro: Myth = sacred story explaining origins, gods, ancestors. Important in understanding religion.

Functions:

Explains natural and social phenomena.

Provides legitimacy to authority (e.g., divine kingship).

Strengthens group identity.


Examples:

Hindu myths (Ramayana, Mahabharata) guide morality.

Greek myths explain gods’ hierarchy.

Tribal myths (creation myths of Andamanese).


Conclusion: Myths are symbolic texts of religion – key to anthropological understanding of worldviews.

Q10 (PYQ 2019 – Paper II). Examine the significance of Bhakti–Sufi movement in promoting religious syncretism in India. (15 marks)

Answer:

Intro: Bhakti and Sufi movements arose (medieval period) against ritualism, emphasized devotion and unity of God.

Bhakti saints: Kabir, Mirabai, Nanak – opposed caste rigidities, stressed love of God.

Sufi saints: Chishti, Suhrawardi – emphasized love, brotherhood, service to poor.

Impact: Blended Hindu and Islamic traditions, created shared cultural space (music, poetry, dargah worship).

Conclusion: Bhakti–Sufi syncretism promoted interfaith harmony and remains crucial to India’s composite culture.
