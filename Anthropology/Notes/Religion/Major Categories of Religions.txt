📌 Major Categories of Religions

1. Abrahamic Religions

Origin: Middle East (West Asia).

Common Link: Traces spiritual lineage to Abraham.

Key Faiths: Judaism, Christianity, Islam.

Traits: Monotheism (belief in one God), scriptures, prophets, salvation history.



2. Dharmic / Indian Religions

Origin: Indian subcontinent.

Key Faiths: Hinduism, Buddhism, Jainism, Sikhism.

Traits: Concepts of dharma (duty/order), karma, rebirth, liberation (moksha/nirvana).



3. Chinese Religions

Origin: Ancient China.

Key Faiths/Philosophies: Confucianism, Taoism, Chinese folk religion.

Traits: Harmony with nature, ancestor veneration, balance of social and cosmic order.



4. Other World Religions & Indigenous Traditions

African Traditional Religions, Shinto (Japan), Zoroastrianism (Persia), Indigenous/animistic faiths.

Traits: Often polytheistic/animistic, tied to land, ancestors, and local communities.

🌍 One-Paragraph Overviews

✡ Judaism

Judaism, the oldest Abrahamic faith, began around 2000 BCE with Abraham’s covenant with God and later the laws revealed to Moses. It emphasizes one God (Yahweh), ethical living, and adherence to the Torah (sacred law). Jewish identity is both religious and ethnic, strongly tied to community and tradition. Followers are called Jews, and key practices include Sabbath observance, dietary laws (kosher), and festivals like Passover. It established the groundwork for both Christianity and Islam.


---

✝ Christianity

Christianity grew out of Judaism in the 1st century CE, centered on the life, death, and resurrection of Jesus Christ, believed by followers to be the Son of God and savior of humanity. Its central message is salvation through faith, love, and grace. The Bible is the sacred scripture, and followers are called Christians. The religion spread widely through the Roman Empire and missionary work, becoming the world’s largest religion, with major branches including Catholicism, Orthodoxy, and Protestantism.


---

☪ Islam

Islam was founded in the 7th century CE in Arabia by Prophet Muhammad, who Muslims believe is the final messenger of God (Allah). The Quran is its holy book, and its core pillars include faith, prayer, charity, fasting, and pilgrimage (Hajj). Followers are called Muslims, and they view themselves as part of a global community (Ummah). Islam emphasizes monotheism, submission to God, and moral guidance, and it spread rapidly across the Middle East, Africa, and Asia.


---

🕉 Hinduism

Hinduism, the oldest Indian religion, evolved over thousands of years with no single founder. It is diverse, encompassing devotion to many deities (like Vishnu, Shiva, Devi), philosophies, and rituals. Central concepts include dharma (duty), karma (actions and consequences), samsara (cycle of rebirth), and moksha (liberation). Its sacred texts include the Vedas, Upanishads, Bhagavad Gita, and epics like the Ramayana and Mahabharata. Followers are called Hindus, and it is deeply tied to India’s cultural and social fabric.


---

☸ Buddhism

Buddhism began in the 5th century BCE with the teachings of Siddhartha Gautama (the Buddha) in India. It rejects caste distinctions and focuses on ending suffering (dukkha) through the Four Noble Truths and the Eightfold Path. Followers, called Buddhists, aim for enlightenment (nirvana)—a release from rebirth and suffering. It spread widely to East and Southeast Asia, developing traditions like Theravāda, Mahāyāna, and Vajrayāna. Meditation, mindfulness, and compassion are central practices.


---

🕊 Jainism

Jainism originated in India around the 6th century BCE, led by Mahavira (24th Tirthankara). It teaches strict nonviolence (ahimsa), truth, and self-discipline as the path to liberation. Jains reject violence toward any living being, leading to vegetarianism and careful ethical living. Followers are called Jains, and they believe in karma, rebirth, and ultimate liberation (moksha) through self-purification and renunciation.


---

⚔ Sikhism

Sikhism arose in 15th-century Punjab, India, founded by Guru Nanak and developed through ten Sikh Gurus. It emphasizes devotion to one God, equality, community service, and honest living. The sacred scripture is the Guru Granth Sahib. Followers are called Sikhs, often identifiable by the 5 Ks (articles of faith). Sikhism rejects caste and ritualism, focusing instead on remembrance of God (Naam), selfless service, and justice.


---

☯ Confucianism

Confucianism is an ethical and philosophical system based on the teachings of Confucius (551–479 BCE) in China. Rather than focusing on gods, it emphasizes moral cultivation, respect for elders, filial piety, education, and maintaining harmony in society. Followers are sometimes called Confucians, though it functions more as a way of life than a religion. It shaped Chinese politics, family structure, and culture for centuries, stressing order and human relationships.


---

☯ Taoism (Daoism)

Taoism, rooted in the teachings of Laozi and texts like the Tao Te Ching, emphasizes living in harmony with the Tao (the Way), the natural order of the universe. Followers (called Taoists) practice meditation, ritual, traditional medicine, and sometimes alchemy to cultivate balance and longevity. Symbolized by the Yin-Yang, Taoism values spontaneity, simplicity, and flow with nature rather than rigid social rules. It coexisted with Confucianism in shaping Chinese culture.


---

🏮 Chinese Folk Religion

This is a broad set of indigenous traditions in China, blending ancestor worship, local deities, and Taoist/Confucian ideas. It lacks a centralized scripture but emphasizes harmony with spirits, rituals for prosperity, and honoring ancestors. Followers don’t have a single name but are often referred to as practitioners of folk religion. Temples, festivals, and rituals keep community and cosmic balance.


---

🔥 Zoroastrianism

Founded in ancient Persia by Prophet Zoroaster (Zarathustra), this faith worships Ahura Mazda as the supreme God. It teaches dualism—the struggle between truth/light and falsehood/darkness. Its scripture is the Avesta. Followers are called Zoroastrians or Parsis (in India). Fire is a sacred symbol, representing purity. Once a dominant world religion, it influenced later Abrahamic traditions.
