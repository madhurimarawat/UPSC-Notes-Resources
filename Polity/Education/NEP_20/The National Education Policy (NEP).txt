The National Education Policy of India 2020 (NEP 2020), which was started by the Union Cabinet of India on 29 July 2020, outlines the vision of new education system of India.[1] The new policy replaces the previous National Policy on Education, 1986.

The National Education Policy (NEP) 2020 significantly alters India's education system, transitioning from a 10+2 structure to a 5+3+3+4 system, emphasizing foundational literacy and numeracy, and introducing flexible learning paths with multiple entry and exit points in higher education. It also promotes mother tongue as the medium of instruction till class 5, with recommendations for its continuation till class 8 and beyond. [1, 2, 3, 4]  
Here's a more detailed breakdown: 
School Education: 

• Restructuring of School Stages: The traditional 10+2 system is replaced with a 5+3+3+4 system, encompassing foundational (5 years), preparatory (3 years), middle (3 years), and secondary (4 years) stages. [1, 3]  
• Early Childhood Care and Education (ECCE): NEP 2020 expands the Right to Education to include 3 years of pre-primary education, integrating Anganwadis. [3]  
• Focus on Foundational Literacy and Numeracy: A National Mission on Foundational Literacy and Numeracy will be established to ensure all students achieve basic reading and math skills by grade 3. [3, 5]  
• Flexibility and Choice: Students will have more subject choices from class 6 onwards, including vocational training, and will be able to choose their learning paths. [3, 5]  
• Multilingualism: Mother tongue or local language is emphasized as the medium of instruction till class 5, with recommendations for its use till class 8 and beyond. [3, 4]  
• Holistic Progress Cards: Traditional report cards are replaced with comprehensive progress cards that include feedback from teachers, peers, and parents, as well as self-evaluation. [6]  
• Assessment Reforms: NEP 2020 proposes a move away from rote memorization towards a more holistic assessment approach, with a focus on formative assessments and projects. [3, 7]  
• Board Exams: There will be two board exams per year, with a focus on continuous evaluation, and the final exam will be less crucial than before. [3]  

Higher Education: 

• Multiple Entry/Exit System: Undergraduate programs will allow students to enter and exit at various stages, receiving certificates or diplomas for completed years of study. [2, 8]  
• Increased GER: The policy aims to increase the Gross Enrolment Ratio (GER) in higher education from 26.3% to 50% by 2035. [9, 10]  
• Academic Bank of Credits: A digital system will track and store the credits earned by students throughout their learning journey. [6]  
• Common Entrance Exams: Common entrance exams (like CUET) will be introduced for college admissions. [3, 6]  
• Integration of Technology: NEP 2020 encourages the use of technology in education, including ERP and LMS software. [11]  
• Global Partnerships: Top international universities may be allowed to set up campuses in India. [3]  
• Four-Year Bachelor's Degree: The policy recommends a four-year bachelor's degree program with multiple exit and entry options. [3, 8]  
• Emphasis on Research: The National Research Foundation will be established to promote research in higher education. [6]  

Other Key Changes: 

• Increased Public Investment: The government aims to increase public investment in education to 6% of GDP. [6, 9]  
• Focus on Teacher Training: NEP 2020 emphasizes teacher training and professional development. [10]  
• Digital Education: The policy encourages online and digital learning methods. [5]  
• Emphasis on Indian Languages: The policy supports the use and promotion of Indian languages in education. [4, 5]
